Six findings that confirm or strongly favor VT over SVT with aberrancy

  • Very wide QRS (>160ms) strongly favors
  • Total concordance (V1-V6 all deflect the same direction) strongly favors
  • AV dissociation (P waves marching independently of QRS) pathognomonic
  • Capture and fusion beats (narrow or hybrid beats during the run) pathognomonic
  • Extreme axis (northwest axis, between -90° and +180°) strongly favors

Pathognomonic for VT (100% specific)

  • AV dissociation
  • Fusion beats (hybrid complexes where sinus and VT impulses collide)
  • Capture beats (a narrow sinus beat sneaks through during the wide complex run)

Strongly favor VT

  • Concordance (positive or negative in V1-V6)
  • Northwest axis (extreme axis deviation)
  • QRS > 160ms (the wider, the more likely VT)
  • Brugada sign (onset of QRS to nadir of S wave >100ms in any precordial lead)
  • Josephson sign (notching near the nadir of the S wave)
  • R-wave peak time >50ms in lead II
  • No RS complex in any precordial lead (all Q/QS, or all R)
  • RS interval >100ms in any precordial lead
  • Lead aVR: initial R wave, R >40ms, or notch on descending limb (Vereckei criteria)
  • Morphology doesn't fit typical RBBB or LBBB (bizarre QRS)
  • History of structural heart disease or prior MI

Favor SVT with aberrancy

  • Typical RBBB pattern (rsR' in V1, wide S in V6)
  • Typical LBBB pattern (narrow r, swift S descent in V1)
  • Previous ECG shows same BBB morphology in sinus rhythm
  • Young patient, no structural heart disease
  • Irregular rhythm (favors AFib with aberrancy over VT)
